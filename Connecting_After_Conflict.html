Connecting After Conflict

Something hard happened between you.
This tool walks you through a 3-sentence repair — grounded in Gottman research — that you can say out loud or share with your person. It won't solve the disagreement. It will restore the connection so you can actually talk later.
This is NOT:
✕ A re-argument in disguise
✕ Defending your position
✕ Pretending it didn't happen
✕ Requiring them to respond a certain way
This IS:
✓ A real, honest repair — in your own words
✓ Reconnecting without solving everything first
✓ Under 5 minutes
Why repair attempts work
Gottman research shows that repair attempts — even imperfect ones — are the single strongest predictor of whether conflict escalates or resolves. The goal is not to fix everything tonight. The goal is to restore emotional safety so that connection — and eventually conversation — can happen.

Before you reach out — check in with yourself.
Gottman research shows that trying to repair when you're flooded (heart rate elevated, thoughts racing) often makes things worse. You don't have to be perfectly calm — just calm enough.
How are you feeling right now?

It makes sense you're still activated. Reaching out when flooded can backfire — you might say something you don't mean, or take back the repair mid-sentence.

Try one of these first: take 5 slow breaths, splash cold water on your face, or step outside for a few minutes. Come back when your body has settled a bit.

Build your 3-sentence repair.
Each sentence has a job. Choose from the options or adjust to sound like you — then watch your script come together below.
1
Acknowledge — without rehashing
Name that something hard happened. Don't re-open the argument. Don't explain yourself. Just let them know you're turning back toward them.
How would you describe what happened between you?
How do you want to reach toward them?
2
Own your part — briefly, without defending
Use an "I" statement about one specific thing — not a character judgment, not a list. The phrase "even if we still see this differently" is intentional: it keeps your repair from reopening the debate. You don't have to be fully resolved to reach back.
I'm sorry for…
Try to name one specific behavior, not a verdict on yourself or them.
Examples: "the tone I used"  ·  "shutting down when you needed me to stay"  ·  "the words I chose"  ·  "not listening as well as I could have"
3
Reach — state a positive need
Gottman's research on the Softened Startup shows that stating what you do need — not what you want them to fix — makes it much easier for your person to actually respond. This sentence is about you reaching, not about putting something on them.
What I need most right now is…

Remember
You are not solving the disagreement tonight. You are restoring the connection — so that when the bigger conversation happens, it can actually go somewhere. The topic can wait. The relationship can't afford to stay disconnected.
If they're not ready to respond yet
That's okay. A repair attempt doesn't require an immediate response to be real. You've reached. Give them time. Most people soften when they feel genuinely reached for — rather than argued at or ignored.

You did something brave.

Reaching back toward someone after conflict — without rehashing, without defending, without pretending — takes real courage. That kind of repair is what keeps relationships alive over time.

What comes next, when you're both ready
The Gottman-Rapoport approach: one person shares their experience fully, without defending. The other listens and summarizes — before anyone problem-solves or persuades. Understanding must come before resolution. Your therapist can guide you through that conversation.
Gottman Research
In Dr. John Gottman's research with thousands of couples, the ability to make — and accept — repair attempts was the clearest predictor of relationship stability. It's not conflict that damages relationships. It's the failure to repair afterward.
