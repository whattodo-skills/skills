Start where you are. Tight chest, stomach pit, shakiness, or nausea can be signs that your body is activated. Slow breathing gives it something steady to follow.

Why slow breathing helps

Slow, diaphragmatic breathing can support the body’s relaxation response. As your breathing slows and your exhale lengthens, your body receives a signal that it can shift out of high alert and toward a calmer state.

Simple science: The vagus nerve is part of the pathway that helps regulate breathing, heart rate, digestion, and the body’s “rest and digest” response. Breathing slowly can gently support that calming pathway.
What to practice: breathe low and slow, let your belly or lower ribs expand, make the exhale slightly longer than the inhale, and keep your shoulders soft.

If breathing exercises make you feel worse, dizzy, or short of breath, stop and return to normal breathing. If you have chest pain, fainting, or symptoms that feel medically urgent, seek immediate help.
