Notice Your Inner World

Describe facts and inner signals before reacting.

Pay attention to what is influencing your behavior.

This practice helps you name thoughts, emotions, body sensations, urges, assumptions, and observable facts without turning them into blame or certainty.

This matters because your inner world can quietly steer what you do next. When you can describe it clearly, you create enough space to choose an action that fits the facts, not only the urge.

Learn the skill

Observe means notice what is happening. Describe means put words on what you noticed.

Use simple phrases: "I notice..." "I am noticing the thought..." "I feel..." "I have an urge to..."

Facts are not stories

A fact is observable. A story is meaning your mind adds. Assumptions are not wrong; they are incomplete information.

Example: "She did not text me back, and I am assuming she is upset with me. I do not know that yet."

Choose your mode

Use Quick Describe during emotionally intense moments. Use Deep Describe when you have more time and want a fuller picture.

Describe what happened

Write what could be seen, heard, recorded, or directly observed.

Fact check
Write what happened to build a cleaner description.

Name what is happening inside

Use labels. You are not arguing with the experience or deciding whether it should be there.

Name the urge and the unknown

You can describe an urge without acting on it. The skill is noticing the pull before it becomes behavior.

Describe phrase
Name at least one inner signal to build a describe phrase.

Choose one action that fits the facts

This is separate from describing. First you notice what is happening; then you choose one response based on the facts, not only the urge.

Next step
Choose one action and when you will begin.

Review what happened

You can use this now if something already happened, or come back later after you practice the action.
