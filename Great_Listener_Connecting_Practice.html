Great Listener Practice

Great listening is not waiting for your turn to talk.

It is the practice of setting your own agenda down for a few minutes so you can understand what another person is experiencing.

This tool helps you prepare, ask better questions, reflect back what you heard, and respond with warmth before advice.

The aim: help the other person feel heard, understood, and less alone — without fixing, correcting, or taking over the conversation.

Prepare yourself first.

Before you listen, shift your attention away from proving, solving, defending, or becoming interesting. Your job is to become interested.

Pick a doorway into the conversation.

Open-ended questions invite more than a yes or no. Choose one to practice with.

Choose a question above.
When you ask it, let the answer breathe. Do not rush to fill the silence.

Listen without taking over.

For the next minute, imagine the other person is answering. Your job is to stay present, notice what they might be feeling, and resist the urge to fix it too quickly.
