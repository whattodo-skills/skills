Managing Distractions: Single Task Focus

The One Task Start When You're Distracted

Big, vague tasks make it hard for your brain to know where to begin. When the task feels unclear or overwhelming, our focus and attention looks for something easier, faster, or more interesting. Enter the experience of distraction.

Managing Distractions: Single Task Focus reduces the task to one visible, physical action so your mind has a clear place to return.

What To Do:

Notice when your attention drifts.

Name the distraction lightly: "phone," "worrying," "planning," "daydreaming" - without judging yourself or starting an inner lecture.

Then guide your eyes or hands back to the next physical action in front of you.

You are not trying to hold perfect focus. You are practicing the return.

Each time you notice, name, and come back, you strengthen your ability to direct attention on purpose and rewire your brain away from automatic distraction.

The skill: Notice the distraction, name it, and gently move focus back to the present moment before avoidance or overreaction takes over.
