Direct Your Attention with Stone Focus Practice

Choose where your attention goes.

Directing your attention means choosing where to place your focus rather than allowing every notification, thought, urge, or demand to pull you in a different direction.

This matters because your attention shapes what you feed, what you react to, and what you are able to do next. When attention gets pulled everywhere, you can lose the present moment, scroll longer than you meant to, feed a worry loop, or react before you have a chance to choose.

This practice gives your mind one simple job: follow a small stone as it slowly sinks through clear water. Each time your attention wanders and you bring it back, you are practicing how to choose your focus on purpose.

Use this when: notifications, thoughts, urges, worries, headlines, or other people's demands keep pulling your attention away from what matters right now.
What it can feel like: your body may settle a little, your breathing may slow, and your mind may feel less scattered. You may notice a small pause between the pull and your next choice.

First, name what keeps pulling your attention.

Pick the closest option. This is not about judging yourself. It is a way to notice the notification, thought, urge, or demand that has been choosing for you.

What to do: choose the stone as your focus target. When your mind wanders, notice the pull, name it lightly, and return your attention to the stone.
