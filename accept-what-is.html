Coping

Accept What Is

Recognize the reality of this moment without approving of it, exaggerating it, or spending all your energy fighting facts that cannot immediately be changed.

Acceptance is not approval.

Acceptance means acknowledging: this is what is happening right now. Once you stop arguing with the fact that something has happened, you may have more energy to decide how you want to respond.

You can accept reality and still: feel upset, disagree, set a boundary, ask for help, work to change the situation, or leave an unsafe situation.
Safety first: Do not use this exercise to delay protective action. When you or someone else is in immediate danger, prioritize safety and appropriate help.

Check in with intensity

How intense does this situation feel right now? 0 means not intense. 10 means extremely intense.

Starting intensity: Not rated

Name the hard-to-accept situation

Use one real situation. There is no perfect wording. You are identifying where the fight with reality is happening.

State the facts

Describe what happened using only information a camera, recording, document, or neutral observer could confirm. Leave out judgments, predictions, blame, and conclusions.

Try this shift: Instead of "They canceled because they do not care," use "They canceled our plans this morning."

Notice what your mind is adding

The mind may predict the future, assign blame, exaggerate consequences, or tell you what should have happened. Notice the thought without treating it as confirmed fact.

Separate knowing from fearing

Name the fight with reality

Which part of reality keeps pulling you into the argument?

I can influence this now

Examples: what I say next, whether I ask a question, take a break, set a boundary, ask for support, or care for myself today.

I cannot change this right now

Examples: what already happened, another person's feelings, someone else's decision, time that has passed, or an answer you have not received.

I do not know yet

Examples: what another person is thinking, what will happen next, whether this is permanent, or how long the problem will last.

Create an acceptance statement

Choose one statement or write your own. Read it slowly. Nothing has to change for the practice to count.

Choose the next steady step

Acceptance is not passivity. It creates space to choose your response based on the situation as it actually is.

Check in again

The number may be lower, higher, or unchanged. Lower intensity is not the only sign this helped. You may also have clearer facts, a next step, or more awareness of what remains uncertain.

Ending intensity: Not rated
