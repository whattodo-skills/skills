Distress Tolerance

What is the STOP Skill?

When your emotions are running the show, your thinking brain goes offline. The STOP skill is a four-step pause that interrupts automatic reactions — before you do something you'll regret or say something you can't take back.

It takes about 30 seconds. It works in arguments, spirals, panic, overwhelm, and those moments when you can feel yourself about to snap.

Use this when you notice: Heart pounding · Urge to send a message you'll regret · Starting to shut down · Voice getting louder · Thoughts racing · You're about to react instead of respond

S
Stop
The moment you notice you're reactive — freeze. Don't text. Don't speak. Don't walk away yet. Just stop moving. Your body wants to act. Don't let it. Not yet.
Say to yourself: "I notice I'm activated. I'm going to stop."
T
Take a Step Back
Physically or mentally create distance. Step away if you can. If not, lean back, feet flat on the floor, and take one slow breath.
Take one breath: breathe in for 4 counts, out for 6. Do it once.
4
Breathe in
O
Observe
Look at the situation from a slight distance. Notice what you're feeling, what triggered it, and what's actually happening — without judging it.
Ask yourself three things:
What am I feeling right now?
What triggered this?
What do I actually want to happen here?
P
Proceed Mindfully
Now move — but from a considered place. What is the most effective action right now? Not the most satisfying. The most effective.
Ask: "What would work best here — for me and for the situation?"

What just happened in your brain

When triggered, your amygdala fires before your prefrontal cortex can process what's happening. The STOP skill gives your thinking brain time to come online. That's neuroscience working for you.
