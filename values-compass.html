Choose What Matters: The Values Compass

Values are the internal GPS that guides your focus, especially when you feel overwhelmed or stressed. While goals are destinations, values describe where your presence and focus is most meaningful to you.

What is a value?

A value is a continuous direction, not a destination. It names what matters enough to deserve your attention, energy, and presence. Think of it like heading West — you never actually "arrive" at West, but you can always check your compass to see if you are still moving in that direction.

Example: If you value Reliability, that value can help you push through the resistance of starting a difficult email. If you value Kindness, it can act as a buffer when you feel the urge to snap at a coworker during a stressful afternoon.

We pay attention to the things that feel important. When your values are clear, you have a way to decide where your focus belongs instead of letting urgency, stress, avoidance, or other people's demands choose for you.

When you are keyed up or stuck, your brain often loses track of the "why" behind your actions. By naming your values, you create a standard to measure your attention and actions against — even when emotions are running high.

The focus skill: pause, ask "What matters here?", choose one value, and put your next small action where that value points.

Values vs. Goals

It is easy to confuse a value with a goal. A goal is "finish the project by Friday." A value is "diligence" or "excellence." If you hit a roadblock, your attention may jump to failure, shame, or panic — but you can still return your focus to diligence by communicating the delay clearly and working steadily.

Goals

  • External outcomes
  • Can be blocked by circumstances
  • You either hit them or you don't
  • "Finish the project by Friday"

Values

  • Internal directions
  • Always accessible to you
  • You can always act in alignment
  • "I act with diligence"

This distinction is vital for maintaining self-trust and focus when life becomes unpredictable. Goals tell you what you hope will happen. Values tell you where to place your attention next.

Daily alignment check

At the end of the day, don't just look at what you checked off your list. Ask: "In what moments today did I give my attention to what matters?" "Where did I live my values?" and "Where did I drift?" This is not self-criticism — it is data collection.

What living by values can feel like: clearer, steadier, less scattered, and more grounded. You may still feel stressed, but you are less likely to feel yanked around by whatever is loudest.
